Page  1/90 Executive Summary  
 
The "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project is a strategic initiative jointly 
launched by PU Internationale Deutsche Schule Sarajevo (IDSS) and PPU 
International Montessori House Sarajevo (IMH). This project establishes an 
exemplary international model  of integrated sustainable education and health 
promotion for children aged 3 to 14 (Kindergarten - 9th grade of primary 
school) in Sarajevo. Through a unique synergy between IDSS, a leader in 
innovative education, and IMH, which applies proven Montessori pedagogy, 
this project offers a holistic and evidence -based response to key local and 
global challenges, including climate change, the need for future -oriented 
education, and strengthening the well -being of children and youth.  
From September 1, 2025 to June 19, 2026, the project will directly engage 194 
children and 36 educators in creating a dynamic, safe, and inclusive learning 
environment in 500 m² of purpose -designed green space, serving as a living 
laboratory and "third tea cher".  
Project vision  is to become a leading regional center of excellence and an 
internationally recognized model that demonstrates how a deep integration of 
environmental awareness, health principles, and advanced pedagogy 
creates resilient, responsible, and engaged future generati ons. The mission is to 
develop, implement, and rigorously evaluate this innovative model, and ensure 
its long -term sustainability and high -quality replicability in different contexts.  
Unique value and innovation  project lies in:  
Deep pedagogical integration  
Application of advanced methodologies (Project -Based Learning - PBL, STEAM, 
Content and Language Integrated Learning - CLIL) mapped to international 
frameworks (Bloom, CASEL, NAAEE), with authentic assessment methods 
focused on 21st century skills.  
Synergies of educational philosophies  
Combining the best practices of the German education system and Montessori 
pedagogy through the prism of sustainable development.  
Innovative tools and concepts  
Development and application of tools such as the "Generation Diary" (for 
longitudinal monitoring), specific diagnostic approaches developed in 
collaboration with experts, and integration of principles from frameworks such 
as Doughnut Economics and Place -Based Education.  

Page  2/90 Infrastructure as an advanced resource  
Establishing a bio -intensive educational garden designed according to the 
principles of sustainability and biophilia, using local materials and advanced 
water management systems, with a long -term maintenance plan.  
A systemic approach to sustainability and replicability  
A detailed long -term financial sustainability plan (including multi -year financial 
projections), the establishment of a "Green Reserve Fund" with a clear 
governance structure, and the creation of a comprehensive "Replication 
Toolkit" as a key outcome for s caling.  
Proactive community engagement  
Structured cooperation programs with external partners (schools, NGOs, 
universities, scouts - "Pofalići" detachment), application of the AA1000SES 
principle, and activation of the "Green Schools Network".  
Key activities  include  
development and implementation of an integrated K -IX curriculum; design 
and construction of an educational garden; professional development of 
teachers; development of teaching materials and digital tools; establishment 
of collaborative programs; and rigor ous monitoring and evaluation (M&E) with 
adaptive management mechanisms and tracking of SMART indicators.  
Expected impact  
includes measurable improvements in children's environmental literacy, health 
indicators, and socio -emotional skills; empowering teachers; creating an 
active support network; and generating a positive social return on investment 
(SROI), the preliminary fra mework of which is an integral part of this proposal.  
The project requires a total budget of EUR 50,000 for the initial implementation 
period (September 2025 - June 2026), as stated in the original specification. A 
detailed financial plan (including multi -annual projections), investor attraction 
strategy and cost analysis are provided in the relevant sections and an nexes. 
The plan is based on a diversified financing strategy (institutional contribution, 
EU funds, donations) and includes a robust risk management plan (including 
climate and financial) and contingency plan.  
"Green School - Green Kindergarten" represents an investment in a sustainable 
future, setting the standard for education that is innovative, relevant and 
responsible. We invite potential partners, donors and investors to join us in 
realizing this initiativ e with significant potential for impact and scaling.  
 

Page  3/90 Introduction  
(Educational community as the foundation of sustainable development)  
 
We live in an unprecedented time, marked by rapid technological progress 
and pervasive digitalization. While these trends offer numerous benefits, they 
also deepen the gap between humanity, especially children and young 
people, and the natural world. The direct, sensory experience of nature – the 
smell of damp earth after rain, the texture of leaves, patiently observing a plant 
grow – is becoming increasingly rare, replaced by mediated, virtual worlds. This 
growing alienation not only represents the loss of a fundamental part of the 
human experience, but also has profound implications for our understanding 
of ecological systems, the interdependence of life, and  the urgent need for 
sustainable practices. In such a context, education has a crucial role, not only 
in transmitting knowledge, but also in shaping the values, attitudes, and 
behaviors that will define our future.  
The "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project is born from this understanding 
and represents more than just a school initiative; it is a strategic response to one 
of the key challenges of our time. Launched through a visionary partnership of 
two leading educational institutions in Sarajevo – PU Internationale Deutsche 
Schule Sarajevo (IDSS) and PPU International Montessori House Sarajevo (IMH) 
– this project aims to create a vibrant, integrated learning model that re -
establishes the essential connection b etween children and nature. It 
transcends the traditional divisions between formal and informal learning, 
between the classroom and the outside world, building bridges – literally and 
figuratively – between generations of students (from 3 to 14 years old),  
between different pedagogical approaches, and between the school and the 
wider community. The central element of the project is the transformation of a 
500 m² shared outdoor space into a dynamic, bio -intensive "living laboratory" 
and "third teacher", a sp ace where learning takes place through work, 
research, and direct experience. The project is not just about planting plants; it 
is about planting seeds of knowledge, responsibility, empathy, and a love of 
learning in the hearts and minds of young people.  
Why is this project important?  
The relevance and necessity of the "Green School - Green Kindergarten" 
project stems from its ability to address multiple needs in an innovative and 
integrated manner:  
 
 

Page  4/90 • Synergy of school and kindergarten - Continuum from play to knowledge  
The project establishes a unique educational continuum. The youngest 
participants (kindergarten children, ages 3 -6) begin their journey through 
sensory exploration in the natural environment, developing a fundamental love 
for nature and adopting basic envi ronmental habits through play and guided 
activities. As they move into classrooms (1st through 9th graders, ages 6 -14), 
their learning becomes more structured, but remains deeply connected to 
hands -on work in the garden. They apply theoretical knowledge in  subjects 
such as biology, chemistry, geography, mathematics and languages in a real -
world context. Equally important, older students develop invaluable leadership 
and empathy skills by taking on the role of mentoring younger children during 
shared workshops ( e.g., planting together, tending to compost, observing 
insects). This interaction not only strengthens understanding of the material, but 
also builds strong social bonds, reduces generational barriers, and creates a 
true sense of community and mutual respect wit hin the entire educational 
community.  
• Holistic approach to upbringing and education  
The project recognizes that learning does not just happen within the four walls 
of the classroom. The green space of the garden becomes an extension of the 
classroom, offering a rich and stimulating environment for the child's overall 
development:  
o For kindergarten children  
The garden is a natural classroom where, through play and exploration, 
gross and fine motor skills (digging, planting, watering), sensory 
perception (touching different textures, smelling flowers and plants) are 
developed, and the foundations for understanding natural cycles and 
ecological awareness are laid. At the same time, the garden is also a 
space for socialization; working together in  the flower beds, sharing tools 
and caring for the plants naturally encourages team spirit, cooperation, 
patience, communication and conflict resolution.  
o For school students  
The garden serves as a living laboratory for interdisciplinary learning. 
Concepts learned in the classroom gain a practical dimension – abstract 
mathematical concepts become concrete through measuring the area 
of flower beds or monitoring plant growth; chemical processes are 
explored through soil pH analysis or the composting process; biological 
concepts are directly observed through the life cycles of plants and 
insects. In addition, older students are given the o pportunity to develop 
leadership and resp onsibility through mentoring younger students, 
planning and carrying out small projects within the garden.  

Page  5/90 • Strengthening ties within the community  
The project is designed to actively break down barriers between the school 
and the community that surrounds it. It envisages a structured and meaningful 
involvement of all key stakeholders:  
o Parentsthey are not just passive observers, but active participants 
through volunteer workshops ("Family Garden Days"), where together with 
their children and teachers they work in the garden, learn new skills and 
contribute to a common goal.  
o Scout troop "Pofalići"brings his specific knowledge and enthusiasm, 
providing technical support in organizing ecological actions and 
activities in nature, and serving as a positive role model for children.  
o Local experts(gardeners, agronomists, biologists, ecologists) and 
representatives of relevant associations are invited as guest lecturers and 
mentors, enriching the project with their knowledge and experience and 
connecting the school with the wider profes sional community.  
This multi -layered connection - vertical (children -adults) and horizontal (school -
kindergarten, students -parents -scouts -experts) - creates a strong support 
network that ensures not only the success of the project, but also its deep roots. 
in the community and long -term sustainability.  
How does the project strengthen the IDSS mission?  
The integration of the "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project directly 
contributes to the realization of the key postulates of the Internationale 
Deutsche Schule Sarajevo mission:  
• A safe and stimulating environment through natural exploration  
The school strives to provide a "safe, supportive and inclusive environment". The 
garden's green space extends this vision beyond the classroom, offering 
children a physically and emotionally safe place to explore, experiment and 
learn through direct conta ct with nature. Outdoor sensory "laboratories" - 
where children can touch different textures of soil and plants, smell flowers and 
herbs, listen to the sounds of nature - become a powerful tool for developing 
emotional intelligence and awareness. The intro duction of authentic German 
cultural content, such as the cultivation of plants characteristic of the DACH 
region (Germany, Austria, Switzerland) or the celebration of traditional nature -
related holidays, further enriches the experience. Through practical activities, 
the German language ceases to be only a subject in the classroom; everyday 
use of German names for plants, tools, and gardening processes allows for 
authentic and contextualized language immersion.  
 

Page  6/90 • Interdisciplinary learning through practical challenges  
The school emphasizes the importance of "critical thinking and problem 
solving." The garden provides countless opportunities to apply these skills to real, 
tangible problems. Mathematical challenges arise when planning the layout 
of flower beds, calculatin g the amount of seeds or water needed, or analyzing 
data on plant growth. Science experiments become a part of everyday life - 
from simply measuring the pH of the soil and observing the effects on plant 
growth, to understanding the processes of photosynthe sis or composting. 
Language skills are developed through keeping a diary of observations, writing 
reports on experiments, or preparing presentations about what has been 
learned, in both German and English. This approach makes learning more 
relevant, engagi ng, and long -lasting.  
• Global awareness and responsibility  
Preparing students for a "different and changing world" requires developing 
global awareness and a sense of responsibility towards the planet. The project 
achieves this on several levels. Activities such as the formation of "Eco -patrols" 
that monitor local  environmental problems (eg air quality, waste 
management) and propose solutions directly empower students to become 
active citizens. Plans to establish international partnership projects with schools 
in Germany or other countries that have similar "green"  initiatives open the door 
to sharing experiences, learning together and understanding global 
environmental challenges from different perspectives.  
How does the project support the Montessori vision?  
The principles of Montessori pedagogy, which are at the heart of the work of 
the International Montessori House Sarajevo, find their natural and fruitful 
application within the "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project:  
• Independence through practical work  
The garden is the perfect embodiment of the Montessori concept of "prepared 
environment". Children are provided with realistic, functional tools (small 
shovels, rakes, watering cans) adapted to their size and strength, allowing them 
to independently comple te meaningful tasks. Clear "responsibility cycles" are 
established – from soil preparation and sowing, through regular watering and 
weeding, to fruit harvesting – which are carefully adapted to the 
developmental capabilities of each age group. A key elemen t is "freedom of 
choice" within the structure; during the defined "green hours", children have the 
opportunity to choose which activities or part of the garden they will engage 
in, following their own interests and developing internal motivation.  
 
 

Page  7/90 • Multi -age cooperation as a social mirror  
The project inherently benefits from the Montessori approach to multi -age 
groups. In the shared space of the garden, interaction between children of 
different ages occurs spontaneously and naturally. Older students, by guiding 
and helping younger ones with simple tasks, not only consolidate their own 
knowledge, but also develop patience, communication and leadership skills. 
Younger children learn by ob serving their elders, acquiring new skills and social 
norms through imitation and cooperation. The garden becomes a dynamic 
social mirror where children learn to respect each other, help each other and 
solve problems in real -life situations.  
• Reflection and self -confidence  
Montessori pedagogy emphasizes the importance of giving the child concrete 
feedback so that he can self -assess his work and progress. The garden offers 
plenty of such opportunities. Through making illustrated "plant growth diaries" 
or creating "project por tfolios" (following the path from seed to fruit), children 
get a visual record of their efforts and achievements. The visible results of their 
work - the emergence of a plant, the appearance of a flower, the ripening of 
a fruit - represent the strongest fe edback and a source of intrinsic satisfaction 
and self -confidence. This process of self -reflection and seeing the direct 
connection between effort and results is key to developing persistence and a 
love of learning.  
Common Ground - Where do philosophies meet?  
Although IDSS and IMH come from different but complementary pedagogical 
traditions, this project is built on solid common foundations and values that both 
institutions share:  
• Comprehensive personality development  
Both schools recognize that education must go beyond mere academic 
achievement. The project actively nurtures emotional development through 
developing empathy and care for living things; physical development through 
outdoor work and the development of moto r skills; and creativity through 
artistic activities inspired by nature. The garden becomes a space where 
intellectual, emotional, social, and physical aspects of development are 
harmoniously integrated.  
• Individualized approach  
Both the German education system and Montessori pedagogy, in their own 
ways, strive to adapt learning to the individual needs and pace of each child. 
The project supports this through tools such as the "Green Workbooks" that offer 
tasks adapted to differen t ages and abilities, and by offering different levels of 

Page  8/90 challenge within the same activities - from simple plant recognition to complex 
science experiments or independent research projects.  
• The community as an extension of teaching  
Both institutions believe in the importance of a strong connection between 
school and family, and the wider community. The project "Green School - 
Green Kindergarten" makes this connection tangible. Through joint workshops, 
family days and the involvement of local experts and volunteers, the garden 
becomes a place of meeting, cooperation and joint learning, effectively 
erasing the boundaries between formal education and community life.  
Transformative effects on the educational ecosystem  
The implementation of this project has the potential to trigger significant and 
positive transformations that reach beyond the direct participants:  
• For IDSS students  
The project offers much more than just learning about nature. It provides an 
authentic context for practicing German, makes STEM subjects (science, 
technology, engineering, mathematics) more relevant and interesting through 
practical applications, and deve lops intercultural competence by comparing 
local ecosystems with those in the DACH region or other parts of the world. 
Students develop teamwork, problem -solving, and critical thinking skills in a 
way that traditional teaching can hardly achieve.  
• For children Montessori House (IMH)  
A garden provides a rich sensory environment that is crucial for early 
development, helping children better integrate information received through 
different senses. Rhythmic, repetitive tasks in the garden (watering, weeding) 
can have a therapeutic effect,  helping to develop self -regulation and 
concentration. By observing the growth cycle, from seed to fruit, children learn 
about cause -and-effect relationships in a concrete way, which is the 
foundation for later scientific understanding of the world.  
• For both institutions and beyond  
The project raises the quality of educational provision, making both institutions 
more attractive and recognizable. It demonstrates a concrete model of how 
sustainability principles can be integrated into the everyday life of a school. The 
success of the project may inspire other schools and kindergartens in Sarajevo 
and Bo snia and Herzegovina to launch similar initiatives, potentially starting a 
broader movement towards "greener" and more sustainable education. It 
strengthens ties with the local community, positioning IDSS and IMH not only as 
educational institutions, but a lso as active centers of sustainable development.  

Page  9/90 What exactly does the project change?  
The project brings tangible changes on several levels:  
• For kindergarten children  
Instead of abstract stories about nature, children get direct, positive contact 
with the earth, plants and animals, developing an understanding of the basics 
of ecology through play and sensory experience. Caring for living beings from 
an early age fosters  empathy, patience and a sense of responsibility. Natural 
materials (leaves, twigs, stones, soil) become an unlimited source for creative 
expression and play.  
• For school students  
Theoretical knowledge in core subjects ceases to be abstract and gains 
practical application in a real -world setting. Students develop concrete skills 
such as planning, teamwork, communication, analytical thinking, and solving 
real-world problems (e.g., ho w to improve soil quality, how to protect plants 
from pests in an ecological way). The experience of mentoring younger 
children builds self -confidence, responsibility, and leadership skills.  
• For educational institutions  
The teaching process becomes more dynamic, interactive and motivating for 
students and teachers. The physical space of schools and kindergartens is 
improved and refined, transforming potentially unused space into a functional, 
aesthetically appealing and e ducational green oasis. Institutions strengthen 
their image in the community, positioning themselves as innovative and socially 
responsible organizations dedicated to sustainable development.  
How does the project achieve long -term effects?  
The sustainability of the project is not left to chance, but is built into its structure 
through several key mechanisms:  
• Integration into curricula  
Gardening and sustainability topics are not an add -on, but become an integral 
part of the regular curriculum in different subjects and grades. Monthly or 
seasonal themes (e.g. "Water", "Composting", "Biodiversity") connect garden 
activities to teaching uni ts, ensuring that the project continues to live on after 
the initial funding expires, as it becomes part of the regular educational 
process. Practical tasks, such as keeping a "Plant Growth Journal" or making a 
herbarium, replace or supplement traditional homework, making learning 
more relevant and lasting.  
 

Page  10/90 • Participatory approach  
From the very beginning, the project insists on the active involvement of all 
stakeholders - children, teachers, educators, parents, scouts and other partners 
- in the process of planning, implementation and evaluation of activities. This 
approach creates a sense of shared ownership and responsibility, which is a 
key prerequisite for long -term success and sustainability. Regular meetings, 
workshops and open channels of communication ensure that the proposals 
and ideas of all involved are heard and appreciat ed, and that the project is 
continuously adapted to the needs of the community.  
• Visualization of changes  
Using tools such as a large "Green Map" of the garden that is updated 
throughout the year, exhibitions of children's work, photos and videos, and 
potentially a digital project diary, makes progress visible and tangible for 
everyone. This not only serves as  documentation and a means of promotion, 
but also has a strong motivational effect, showing children and the community 
the concrete results of their efforts and encouraging them to continue their 
engagement.  
Why is now the right time for this project?  
The launch of the "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project right now is not 
only desirable, but also necessary. We live in a time of escalating ecological 
crisis, where education for sustainable development and raising environmental 
awareness from an early age have become a global imperative. At the same 
time, the increasing e xposure of children to digital technologies and the 
reduction of time spent in nature are leading to a phenomenon known as 
"nature deficit disorder", with negative consequences for physical and mental 
health, as well as cognitive and social development. Th is project offers a 
concrete, positive and proactive solution, creating space for direct experience 
of nature, strengthening social ties within communities weakened by the 
pandemic and other social pressures, and developing practical skills and 
knowledge n ecessary for life in the 21st century. It is an investment in a more 
resilient, healthier and more responsible future generation.  
A vision of shared growth  
"Green School - Green Kindergarten" is not intended as a finished story, but as 
a catalyst and starting point for continued growth and development. The vision 
extends far beyond the initial project period and includes:  
• Establishing a long -term, self -sustaining environmental education program 
that will become a permanent part of the identity of IDSS and IMH.  

Page  11/90 • Potential expansion of physical space and scope of activities in the future, 
in accordance with needs and possibilities.  
• Actively seeking and developing international cooperation with related 
schools and organizations, especially in Germany, for the purpose of 
exchanging knowledge, experiences and joint projects.  
• Positioning the project as a regional model of good practice and a resource 
center for other educational institutions in Bosnia and Herzegovina that wish to 
implement similar programs, through dissemination of results, organization of 
trainings and develop ment of the "Replication Toolkit".  
• Initiating and nurturing the "Green Schools Network" in BiH, creating a 
platform for cooperation, support and advocacy for sustainable education at 
the systemic level.  
In essence, just as IDSS strives to provide top -notch modern education, and IMH 
is dedicated to nurturing the development of versatile skills in children, the 
"Green School - Green Kindergarten" project becomes their shared, living 
laboratory - a space whe re the true values of sustainability, cooperation, and 
lifelong learning are not only taught, but also lived and experienced daily, 
building a solid foundation for a brighter and greener future.

1.7 Risk management  
A proactive and systematic approach to risk management is essential for 
minimizing potential negative impacts on the achievement of project 
objectives. The approach is based on the international standard ISO 31000 (Risk 
Management – Principles and Guidelines) and includes a continuous process 
of identifying, analyzing, evaluating, treating, monitoring and communicating 
risks. 
• Access  
Integrated, structured, context -specific, inclusive (involves stakeholders), 
dynamic (continuously updated) and focused on improvement.  
• Process  
1. Risk identification  
Through team brainstorming, analysis of similar projects, and consultations with 
stakeholders, potential risks in various categories (strategic, operational, 
financial, reputational, environmental/climate, pedagogical) are identified.  
2. Risk analysis  
Assessment of the probability (Probability - P) and impact (I) of each identified 
risk (e.g. scale 1 -5). 
3. Risk evaluation  
Determining the level of risk (Risk = P x I) and prioritizing risks for treatment.  
 
 

Page  23/90 4. Risk treatment  
Defining strategies for responding to priority risks (avoidance, transfer, 
mitigation/reduction, acceptance). Developing specific preventive and 
corrective measures.  
5. Monitoring and review  
Regular monitoring of risk status and effectiveness of treatment measures, 
updating of risk register (at least quarterly).  
6. Communication and consultations  
Transparent communication about risks with the Management Board and 
relevant stakeholders.

Page  25/90 2. Project Description  
 
The "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project is a comprehensive initiative 
aimed at creating a dynamic and sustainable educational ecosystem within 
PU Internationale Deutsche Schule Sarajevo (IDSS) and PPU International 
Montessori House Sarajevo (IMH). Through carefully designed activities, 
development of dedicated infrastructure and strong partnerships with the 
community, the project aims to transform the way children learn about the 
environment, health and sustainability, and to lay the foundation for long -term 
positive changes in their attitudes and behaviors. This description elaborates in 
detail the objectives, expected outcomes, key activities and integration 
strategy into the existing curriculum.  
2.1. Project Objectives  
Project objectives are clearly defined to guide all activities towards achieving 
the desired purpose and long -term impact. They include a general objective 
that reflects the broader ambition of the project, and specific objectives that 
specify the concrete  changes to be achieved.  
a. Overall Goal  
The overall goal of the "Green School - Green Kindergarten" project is to, 
through the synergy of education, health promotion and ecological practices, 
transform IDSS and IMH into recognized centers of excellence for sustainable 
development within the Muni cipality of Centar Sarajevo and beyond, 
contributing to the upbringing of generations of young people who are 
environmentally aware, socially responsible, health literate and actively 
engaged in creating a sustainable future.  
b. Specific Objectives  
To achieve the general objective, the following specific objectives have been 
defined and will be achieved by the end of the implementation phase (June 
2026):  
1. Increase environmental awareness, knowledge and skills among children 
and students  
Ensure that all children (3 -6 years old) and students (6 -14 years old) acquire 
fundamental knowledge of key ecological principles (ecology, biodiversity, 
climate change, sustainable resources), develop practical skills for sustainable 
living and adopt posi tive attitudes towards environmental conservation.  
 

Page  26/90 2. Develop and apply practical skills for sustainable living  
Enable children and students to acquire and apply specific skills such as 
organic planting and growing plants, composting, recycling, efficient use of 
water and energy, waste reduction and understanding the importance of 
renewable energy sources.  
3. Integrate environmental, health and sustainability topics into the curriculum  
Systematically include topics related to the environment, health and 
sustainable development in all relevant subjects and grades (K -IX), using an 
innovative, experiential and interdisciplinary approach that connects theory 
with practice.  
4. To create a high -quality, stimulating and multifunctional environment for 
learning about the environment and health  
Arrange and equip a school garden (500m²), a greenhouse, a botanical 
terrace and an eco -playground as inspiring and safe spaces for outdoor 
learning, experimentation, nature exploration and the promotion of physical 
activity.  
5. Strengthen the capacities of educators to implement integrated sustainable 
education  
To empower teachers and educators (36 of them) with the necessary 
knowledge, skills and resources for the effective application of innovative 
pedagogical methods (PBL, STEAM, CLIL, Inquiry -Based Learning) in the context 
of sustainable development and healt h. 
6. Improve the health well -being and socio -emotional competences of 
children  
Encourage the adoption of healthy lifestyle habits (proper nutrition, physical 
activity, mental health) and develop key socio -emotional skills (empathy, 
cooperation, self -regulation, responsible decision -making) through project 
activities.  
7. Promote and strengthen cooperation and partnerships within the community  
Actively involve parents, the "Pofalići" Scout Troop, the local community, NGOs 
and businesses in the implementation of the project, creating a strong support 
network for sustainable initiatives and active citizenship.  
8. Develop and test a model for monitoring, evaluation, sustainability and 
replication  

Page  27/90 Establish a robust M&E system, ensure mechanisms for long -term financial and 
operational sustainability (including a "Green Reserve Fund"), and create and 
validate a framework for the dissemination of the model ("Replication Toolkit").
